I've been using several AI tools and services heavily recently — some pleasantly surprised, some deeply frustrating. Here's the unfiltered experience.

## Multica: Master of Cyber Livestock Management

Multica's recent updates are genuinely impressive. The development pace has always been fast, and now they've added **scheduled tasks** and **team features**. You can set up timed triggers to let agents run autonomously and collaborate with teammates. No more manually directing your "cyber livestock" — just schedule the work and let it run. Management efficiency has gone up a full notch.

For indie developers, Multica's approach as an "AI-native task management platform" feels ahead of its time. I no longer need to juggle multiple tools — coding, issue tracking, and pipelines all in one place.

## MiniMax: High Hopes, Underwhelming Reality

MiniMax's models come with impressive marketing, but in practice, the gap with DeepSeek V4 is still significant.

First, **context length**. When processing long documents, MiniMax often loses track of information in the middle. With DeepSeek V4's million-token context window, you can throw in an entire book and it still finds the exact detail you need.

Second, **answer accuracy**. For anything involving multi-step reasoning or logic chains, MiniMax tends to go off the rails, while DeepSeek V4 handles it consistently well.

Not saying MiniMax is bad — it's decent. But comparing it to the current domestic leader DeepSeek, there's still a way to go.

## Google One: Surprisingly Worth It

Here's one I actually want to praise. The Google One subscription now bundles **NotebookLM** and **multimodal Gemini** access. For anyone who reads academic papers regularly, it's a godsend.

NotebookLM handles PDF papers exceptionally well — upload a paper, and it generates summaries, extracts key information, and answers specific questions about the content. Multimodal Gemini can analyze charts and images directly. One subscription covering paper writing, literature review, and research — the value proposition is solid.

## Gemini-cli: A KPI Product?

Now for the mandatory rant. The Gemini model itself is pretty good, but its CLI tool is embarrassingly half-baked.

**Tool calling** is unreliable — calls frequently go unanswered or return malformed output. **Output speed** is also poor; compared to similar streaming tools, it's noticeably slower. The whole experience screams "the model is great, but the CLI was a rush job."

In short, it feels like a product thrown together by a team chasing quarterly OKRs. It works, technically. But "good" is still a million miles away.

---

These are my honest takeaways from recent AI tooling adventures. Some hits, some misses. Hope they all keep improving.
