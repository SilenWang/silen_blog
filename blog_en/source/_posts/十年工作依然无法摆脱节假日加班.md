I graduated in 2017. It's 2026 now. Almost ten years.

And today, Dragon Boat Festival, I'm still working overtime.

<!-- more -->

## Ten Years

It's kind of pathetic when you think about it. When I first started out a decade ago, I told myself "it'll get better once I have more experience," "it'll settle down once I'm established," "it'll be fine once I hit senior level" — always convincing myself that things would improve after getting through just one more phase.

Spoiler: they didn't. Ten years in, the overtime never stopped. Weekends? Holidays? Don't make me laugh. Getting compensatory time off already counts as a generous employer in my book.

## Then Came AI

The last couple of years have been all about AI and Agents — large language models, autonomous agents, automation tools everywhere. The promises were grand: "liberate productivity," "free humanity from repetitive labor," "let AI do the grunt work."

And I do use them. AI helps me write code. Agents automatically analyze and debug. Even this blog post is being drafted with opencode's help. My efficiency is up. My output is way up.

But here's the catch — **the work didn't decrease. It increased.**

Because now that I'm faster, expectations are higher. Used to be I could ship one feature a week. Now I can ship one a day. So naturally, management expects five a week. You've got AI, right? You're a "senior engineer," right?

AI compressed the time per task, but it didn't reduce the number of tasks — on the contrary, because "you're faster now," the workload only grows.

## The Cyber Workhorse Paradox

Before, I was a workhorse. Now I have a team of cyber workhorses helping me. Sounds great on paper, right?

In reality, I've become a **workhorse that manages other cyber workhorses**.

I review the code AI writes. I verify the analyses Agents produce. I edit the documents automation generates — and when something goes wrong, the buck still stops with me. My role shifted from "doing the work myself" to "cleaning up after others/AI." The net workload barely changed.

The most ironic part? Because AI can do more, management assumes we can deliver more. Project complexity, delivery velocity, the number of concurrent tasks — all rising exponentially.

This isn't "tools liberating humanity." This is "tools cranking up the intensity."

## When Will It End

Almost a decade in, I've long given up on "work-life balance." I just sometimes wonder, during those holiday work sessions — is the world actually getting better, or just getting more intense?

AI and Agents are genuinely powerful. They write code, analyze data, automate workflows. But they can't solve the fundamental question of "why is there never enough time to finish everything?" Because that problem isn't about tools — it's about organizations and expectations. An efficient tool doesn't get you home early; it just lets people expect more from you.

Maybe this is what they call a mid-life crisis. Not that your body can't keep up anymore, but that your spirit can't.

Anyway, back to work.

Dragon Boat zongzi tastes fine cold, I guess.
